A nonprofit's twelve-year effort to give young people the space to speak honestly about race and culture is producing results that cannot be ignored — and a 94% satisfaction rate that rivals any program in the country.

By IMU Community Desk  ·  Reported from Chicago, Los Angeles and Atlanta  ·  Monday, March 30, 2026

In a gymnasium in Chicago on a Thursday afternoon, fifteen students arrange their chairs into a circle. There are no desks between them. No teacher at the front of the room. For the next hour, this space is theirs.

This is an Inspiring Multicultural Understanding dialogue circle, and it has been happening — in variations, in schools from Los Angeles to Atlanta — for twelve years. What began as a single weekly session at one Chicago middle school in 2014 has grown into a nationwide program serving more than 4,200 students across 38 partner schools in 12 cities.

The organization's core premise is disarmingly simple: that young people need safe, structured spaces to talk honestly about culture, race, and identity. Not as abstract topics, but as the lived realities of the people sitting beside them.

"Most schools treat these conversations like dangerous territory," says the IMU program director. "We treat them like necessary territory. Like the most important conversation a school could possibly make room for."

The data appears to support that conviction. In post-program surveys conducted across all 38 partner schools, 94 percent of students reported feeling more understood by their peers after completing an IMU program. Eighty-eight percent said they were more comfortable discussing their own cultural identity. Seventy-nine percent reported a greater sense of belonging at school overall.

Perhaps most striking: 97 percent said they would recommend the program to a friend.

"We don't hand students a curriculum. We ask them what they need — and then we build it together."
— IMU Program Director, Chicago

How It Works

Each IMU program pairs students with a trained facilitator — often a peer from the same school — for a series of structured dialogue sessions. Topics emerge from students themselves: identity, belonging, cultural heritage, the experience of being misunderstood.

No topic is ruled out. No student is silenced. The only rule: speak for yourself.

Personal Account

'I Realized I Was Never as Alone as I Thought'

By Maya R., 17  ·  IMU Participant

Before IMU, I never talked about being biracial at school. I thought no one would understand. I had learned, without anyone saying it directly, that certain parts of who I was were safer left unmentioned.

In my first dialogue circle, a girl I barely knew started crying because she felt the exact same way. We became best friends. That is what IMU does — it makes you realize you are never as alone as you think. It sounds small when you say it. It is not small when you feel it.

"It makes you realize you are never as alone as you think."
— Maya R., 17 · Chicago, Illinois
Student Report

'My Culture Finally Had a Place Here'

By Javier M., 16  ·  IMU Student Leader

I grew up in a mostly white school and never saw my culture reflected anywhere. Not in the books. Not in the curriculum. Not in the conversations teachers chose to have or to avoid.

IMU was the first time I heard my language and my traditions spoken about with real respect. Not as a curiosity. Not as something to be explained to other people. Just as part of the fabric of the room. I finally felt like I belonged — not despite who I am, but because of it. That difference is everything.

From Fear to Facilitator: The Story of Aisha T.

By The Student Record  ·  Atlanta Bureau

Two years ago, Aisha T. was afraid to bring up race in class. Now, at 18, she runs dialogue circles at her own school in Atlanta — sessions she designed, facilitated, and invited her entire district to attend.

"IMU trained me to lead conversations I once avoided entirely," she says. "I watch students open up in ways no textbook ever could." This past spring, Aisha organized her school's first student-led dialogue forum. The district superintendent attended. Three principals called the following week. This is what the Youth Facilitator Program was always meant to become.

"This program didn't just change my school," Aisha says. "It changed who I am."

"A student who feels unseen cannot learn. Cannot lead. Cannot become who they were meant to be. That is why we exist — and why, after twelve years, we are not done."

How We Work
The Four Pillars of the IMU Program
Pillar I

Open Dialogue

Safe, facilitated spaces for honest conversation about race, culture, and identity — structured to build trust before depth. Every session begins with a shared agreement: to speak from personal experience, to listen without judgment, and to hold what is said in confidence.

Pillar II

Student Voice

Programs are co-designed with the young people who participate in them. Students are not the audience for IMU's curriculum — they are its authors. Their lived experiences, questions, and concerns shape every session from the ground up. The facilitator guides. The students lead.

Pillar III

Educator Partnership

IMU works directly with teachers and administrators to embed inclusion into the daily fabric of school life — not as a module that comes and goes, but as a permanent feature of how a school functions.

Pillar IV

Youth Leadership

Students trained as facilitators carry the work forward long after IMU departs. More than 420 young people have completed facilitator training since 2014 — each one a multiplier, running circles in their own schools and communities.
