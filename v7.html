To whoever is reading this —

I want to tell you about the day I finally said something out loud that I had been carrying around in silence for years.

I was sitting in a circle with fourteen other students — people I passed in the hallway every day without really seeing. And someone asked a question that no teacher had ever asked me before. She asked: "What part of who you are do you leave at the door when you walk into school?"

★ I didn't answer right away. I looked around the room. And then — one by one — I watched every single person in that circle start to answer. Quietly, then a little louder. Some of them cried. I cried. And it felt like something that had been held very tightly finally let go.

That was my first IMU dialogue circle. I was sixteen.

My name is Aisha. I'm eighteen now, and I'm writing this because I want you to understand what IMU — Inspiring Multicultural Understanding — actually is. Not in the way a brochure explains it. In the way it felt to be a student who needed it.

It is a youth nonprofit. It's been running for twelve years. It works with schools to create spaces where students can talk honestly about culture, race, and identity — things that shape every single day of a young person's life, but that most schools treat like dangerous territory.

this. ↑ IMU doesn't treat it like dangerous territory. IMU treats it like necessary territory. Like the most important conversation a school could possibly make room for.

But the number I think about most is not on that list. It's the number of students who walked into a room feeling invisible and walked out feeling, for the first time, like themselves.

I want you to hear it from some of them directly:

"Before IMU, I never talked about being biracial at school. I thought no one would understand. In my first dialogue circle, a girl I barely knew started crying because she felt the exact same way. We became best friends."

— Maya, 17  ·  Chicago, IL

"I grew up in a mostly white school and never saw my culture reflected anywhere. IMU was the first time I heard my language and my traditions spoken about with real respect. I finally felt like I belonged — not despite who I am, but because of it."

— Javier, 16  ·  Los Angeles, CA

I could keep going. I have a hundred more of these. Every single one of them is a real person who sat in a circle and found something they didn't know they were missing.

Here is what I need to ask you:

I was lucky. My school said yes to IMU. A lot of schools — most schools — have not said yes yet. And the only thing standing between those students and a circle like the one I sat in is resources.

Which means it's partly standing between them and you.

I know that sounds like a lot to put on a stranger. But you're reading this letter. That means something to me. It means you're already paying attention. And paying attention is how everything that ever mattered actually started.

If you can give something — anything — please do. And if you can't, then share this. Tell someone. Say IMU out loud to one person who might care. That matters too.

Every student deserves to be seen. I really, genuinely believe that. I hope after reading this, you do too.

With everything I have,

Aisha, Maya, Javier,
